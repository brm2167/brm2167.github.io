What are QR codes?

QR codes have been around since the 1990s, but they gained significant popularity in recent years, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic, as demand for contactless interactions surged. These two-dimensional barcodes encode binary data, typically web URLs, into a format that can be read by image sensors, such as the camera on your smartphone.

What are the risks?

The danger with QR codes lies in the fact that they're designed to be read by image sensors, not humans. When you click on a link, you can see the URL and assess whether it looks trustworthy — like recognizing that www.amazon.com is safe, while www.ama.zondeals.net raises red flags. However, with a QR code, you have no way of knowing where it leads until you scan it. Worse still, even if a QR code comes from a reliable source, malicious actors can easily cover it with their own altered code, which can be hard to spot unless you're actively looking for tampering.


Once you scan a malicious QR code, it can open the door to a variety of threats. The website may impersonate a legitimate one, prompting you to enter sensitive information like login credentials, credit card details, or other personal data. Additionally, it could infect your device with viruses, ransomware, or other malicious software that can slow down your computer, steal valuable data, or lock your device until a ransom is paid. You may also face a barrage of intrusive ads that can lead to unwanted software installations or send you to other harmful websites, putting your device and privacy at risk.

How to spot phishing attempts

To avoid falling victim to phishing, it's crucial to practice good internet hygiene and stay vigilant. Phishing campaigns, like the one you encountered here, often rely on creating a sense of urgency to trick you into acting quickly without thinking. Whether it's an offer that seems too good to be true, a deadline that's looming, or a warning about your account being compromised, scammers know that pressure can cloud judgment. If you're being pushed to act immediately — especially if the request seems unexpected or unusual — this is a major red flag. Always take a moment to pause, verify the source, and consider whether the situation truly requires urgent action. Trusting your instincts and taking the time to double-check can save you from falling into a phishing trap.
